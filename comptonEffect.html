Complete Derivation of the Compton Effect

1. Physical Setup

Consider a photon with initial wavelength λ colliding with a stationary electron (rest mass me). After collision:

  • Photon is scattered at angle θ with wavelength λ'
  • Electron recoils at angle φ with momentum pe

This interaction demonstrates the particle nature of light while obeying conservation laws.

2. Relativistic Energy of Recoiling Electron

The electron's total energy after collision is given by the relativistic energy-momentum relation:

\[ E_e' = \sqrt{(p_e c)^2 + (m_e c^2)^2} \]

Where:

  • pe = electron's momentum after collision
  • me = electron rest mass (9.11 × 10-31 kg)
  • c = speed of light (2.998 × 108 m/s)

3. Conservation of Energy

Initial Energy (Before Collision)

Photon energy: \( E_\gamma = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \)

Electron energy (at rest): \( E_e = m_e c^2 \)

Total initial energy:

\[ E_{\text{initial}} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} + m_e c^2 \]

Final Energy (After Collision)

Scattered photon energy: \( E_\gamma' = h\nu' = \frac{hc}{\lambda'} \)

Recoiling electron energy: \( E_e' = \sqrt{(p_e c)^2 + (m_e c^2)^2} \)

Total final energy:

\[ E_{\text{final}} = \frac{hc}{\lambda'} + \sqrt{(p_e c)^2 + (m_e c^2)^2} \]

Energy Conservation Equation

\[ \frac{hc}{\lambda} + m_e c^2 = \frac{hc}{\lambda'} + \sqrt{(p_e c)^2 + (m_e c^2)^2} \]

4. Conservation of Momentum

Initial Momentum

Photon momentum: \( p_\gamma = \frac{h}{\lambda} \) (along x-axis)

Electron momentum (at rest): \( p_e = 0 \)

Final Momentum

Photon momentum after scattering:

\( p_\gamma' = \frac{h}{\lambda'} \) at angle θ

Components:

\[ p_{\gamma x}' = \frac{h}{\lambda'} \cos \theta \quad \text{(x-component)} \] \[ p_{\gamma y}' = \frac{h}{\lambda'} \sin \theta \quad \text{(y-component)} \]

Electron momentum: \( p_e \) at angle φ

Components:

\[ p_{e x} = p_e \cos \phi \quad \text{(x-component)} \] \[ p_{e y} = p_e \sin \phi \quad \text{(y-component)} \]

Momentum Conservation Equations

x-direction:

\[ \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{h}{\lambda'} \cos \theta + p_e \cos \phi \]

y-direction:

\[ 0 = \frac{h}{\lambda'} \sin \θ - p_e \sin \phi \]

5. Solving the Equations

Step 1: Solve for pe

From y-momentum equation (Eq. 6):

\[ p_e \sin \phi = \frac{h}{\lambda'} \sin \θ \]

From x-momentum equation (Eq. 5):

\[ p_e \cos \phi = \frac{h}{\lambda} - \frac{h}{\lambda'} \cos \θ \]

Square both equations and add them:

\[ p_e^2 \sin^2 \phi + p_e^2 \cos^2 \phi = \left(\frac{h}{\lambda'} \sin \θ\right)^2 + \left(\frac{h}{\lambda} - \frac{h}{\lambda'} \cos \θ\right)^2 \]

Simplify using sin²φ + cos²φ = 1:

\[ p_e^2 = \left(\frac{h}{\lambda'}\right)^2 \sin^2 \θ + \left(\frac{h}{\lambda} - \frac{h}{\lambda'} \cos \θ\right)^2 \]

Expand the right side:

\[ p_e^2 = \frac{h^2}{\lambda'^2} \sin^2 \θ + \frac{h^2}{\lambda^2} - \frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} \cos \θ + \frac{h^2}{\lambda'^2} \cos^2 \θ \]

Combine terms (sin²θ + cos²θ = 1):

\[ p_e^2 = \frac{h^2}{\lambda^2} + \frac{h^2}{\lambda'^2} - \frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} \cos \θ \]

Step 2: Relate to Energy Equation

From energy conservation (Eq. 3):

\[ \sqrt{(p_e c)^2 + (m_e c^2)^2} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - \frac{hc}{\lambda'} + m_e c^2 \]

Square both sides:

\[ (p_e c)^2 + (m_e c^2)^2 = \left(\frac{hc}{\lambda} - \frac{hc}{\lambda'} + m_e c^2\right)^2 \]

Expand the right side:

\[ (p_e c)^2 = \frac{h^2 c^2}{\lambda^2} + \frac{h^2 c^2}{\lambda'^2} - \frac{2h^2 c^2}{\lambda \lambda'} + \frac{2h m_e c^3}{\lambda} - \frac{2h m_e c^3}{\lambda'} + m_e^2 c^4 - m_e^2 c^4 \]

Simplify:

\[ p_e^2 = \frac{h^2}{\lambda^2} + \frac{h^2}{\lambda'^2} - \frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} + \frac{2h m_e c}{\lambda} - \frac{2h m_e c}{\lambda'} \]

Set equal to Eq. 7:

\[ \frac{h^2}{\lambda^2} + \frac{h^2}{\lambda'^2} - \frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} \cos \θ = \frac{h^2}{\lambda^2} + \frac{h^2}{\lambda'^2} - \frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} + \frac{2h m_e c}{\lambda} - \frac{2h m_e c}{\lambda'} \]

Cancel common terms:

\[ -\frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} \cos \θ = -\frac{2h^2}{\lambda \lambda'} + \frac{2h m_e c}{\lambda} - \frac{2h m_e c}{\lambda'} \]

Divide both sides by 2h:

\[ -\frac{h}{\lambda \lambda'} \cos \θ = -\frac{h}{\lambda \lambda'} + \frac{m_e c}{\lambda} - \frac{m_e c}{\lambda'} \]

Multiply through by λλ':

\[ -h \cos \θ = -h + m_e c \lambda' - m_e c \lambda \]

Rearrange terms:

\[ m_e c (\lambda' - \lambda) = h (1 - \cos \θ) \]

Final Result: Compton Wavelength Shift

\[ \boxed{\Delta \lambda = \lambda' - \lambda = \frac{h}{m_e c} (1 - \cos \θ)} \]

Where:

  • \(\frac{h}{m_e c}\) = Compton wavelength of electron ≈ 0.00243 nm
  • θ = scattering angle of photon

6. Physical Interpretation

  • The wavelength shift depends only on the scattering angle θ
  • Maximum shift occurs at θ = 180° (backscattering): Δλ = 2h/mec ≈ 0.00486 nm
  • No shift occurs at θ = 0° (forward scattering)
  • The electron's recoil energy comes from the photon's energy loss

This effect was first observed by Arthur Compton in 1923 and provided crucial evidence for the particle nature of light.
